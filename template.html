Scripture

"For I know the plans I have for you" — this is the Lord's declaration — "plans for your well-being, not for disaster, to give you a future and a hope."

Jeremiah 29:11 · CSB

This verse gets quoted a lot at graduations and in tough seasons, and it's easy for it to lose its weight. But the original context is striking — Jeremiah is writing to Israelites in exile, people who had lost everything and were living in a foreign land. God's message wasn't "things are about to turn around immediately." It was: stay faithful, build a life where you are, and trust the longer arc. That's a harder and more honest kind of hope than the bumper sticker version.

Stoic reflection

"You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength."

— Marcus Aurelius · Meditations

Worth remembering that Aurelius wasn't writing this from a quiet study — he was running an empire during a plague while managing wars on multiple fronts. The point wasn't philosophical. It was practical. You can't control what happens today, but you get to decide what it means and how you respond. That's the whole game.

Health & longevity

Stop waiting to get sick before you do something about it
Attia's core argument is that modern medicine shows up after you're already in trouble. His approach — he calls it Medicine 3.0 — is to figure out what you're most likely to die from based on your specific biology and family history, then work against it aggressively in your 40s and 50s rather than your 70s. The goal isn't just a longer life. It's staying capable of doing what matters most, right up until the end.

Heart disease, cancer, diabetes, and Alzheimer's all share the same root causes
These four conditions account for the vast majority of premature deaths in the developed world. What most people don't realize is that they share upstream drivers: insulin resistance, chronic inflammation, and oxidative stress. The same lifestyle choices that protect your heart are also protecting your brain. You're not fighting four separate battles — just one.

VO2 max measures how efficiently your body uses oxygen during hard exercise. Going from the bottom quartile to the top for your age roughly halves your 10-year mortality risk. 04 /
Build muscle in your 40s like you're saving for retirement — you'll spend it in your 80s
Muscle loss accelerates sharply after 60 and is very hard to reverse. Attia frames strength training in midlife as depositing reserves you'll draw on decades later. His benchmark is what he calls the "Centenarian Olympics" — the physical tasks you'll need to do independently at 90: getting up off the floor, carrying groceries, climbing stairs without the railing. Train for those now.
05 /
No supplement, diet, or workout routine can compensate for bad sleep — full stop
Attia is blunt about this: sleep is the lever that controls everything else. During sleep, the brain clears metabolic waste, the body releases growth hormone, and emotional memories are processed. Chronically shortchanging it undermines every other health effort you make. He tracks his sleep with the same rigor he tracks his training and labs.
06 /
Emotional health isn't a soft add-on — it's a hard longevity variable
The section of the book that surprised most readers: Attia treats therapy, relationships, and sense of purpose as measurable longevity factors. Chronic psychological distress raises inflammation, disrupts sleep, and drives destructive behaviors. He's candid that his own work in this area — including intensive therapy — changed his life more than any physical health change he made.

Today: Raw Honey
Raw Honey
Ancient food Evidence: Strong

Honey has been eaten by humans for at least 8,000 years — cave paintings in Spain show people harvesting wild hives. It's one of the oldest foods in the human diet. But most of what's sold as "honey" today looks almost nothing like what people were eating for millennia. Here's what raw honey actually is, what it does, and whether it earns a place in a whole-foods diet.

What makes raw honey different

Commercial honey is heated and filtered to make it clear and shelf-stable. That process destroys enzymes like diastase and glucose oxidase, removes pollen and propolis, and wipes out most of the polyphenols that give raw honey its biological activity. What's left is essentially flavored sugar syrup. Raw honey — minimally processed, unheated — retains all of this. It looks cloudier, crystallizes faster, and tastes more complex. That complexity is the point.

What the research actually shows

Raw honey has a legitimate antimicrobial track record — its hydrogen peroxide content, low pH, and high sugar concentration create an environment most bacteria can't survive in. This is why it's been used on wounds for thousands of years and why the research on manuka honey for wound healing is actually solid. Beyond antimicrobial properties, raw honey contains polyphenols linked to reduced oxidative stress and inflammation in several well-designed trials. It also has a modestly lower glycemic impact than refined sugar, partly because fructose is metabolized differently and partly because the polyphenols seem to slow glucose absorption slightly.

The honest caveats

Honey is still predominantly sugar — about 80% by weight. If you're eating it in meaningful quantities expecting a supplement-level effect, you're also taking in significant fructose and glucose. The research on its benefits is real but should be understood in the context of modest doses. Never give raw honey to infants under 12 months — the risk of botulism is well-established and serious. And "raw" on a label isn't regulated in most countries, so sourcing from a local beekeeper or a brand that publishes lab testing is worth the effort.

The bottom line

If you're going to use a sweetener, raw honey from a trusted source is probably the most defensible whole-food option. It has thousands of years of human use behind it, a real (if modest) nutritional profile that refined sugar lacks entirely, and active compounds that do things sugar simply doesn't. Use it the way traditional cultures did — sparingly, as a condiment or medicine — not as a primary calorie source.

Cell Metabolism · Published May 28, 2026
Eating in a shorter daily window preserves more muscle during a diet — even when protein is the same
TL;DR
In a 16-week trial of 240 adults, people who ate all their food within an 8-hour window lost the same amount of fat as those spreading meals across 12 hours — but held onto significantly more muscle. Same total calories, same protein intake. Just a different eating window. The leading explanation: during the extended fasting period, the body gets better at clearing out damaged cellular material rather than cannibalizing structural muscle. It's a meaningful finding for anyone cutting calories while trying to preserve the muscle they've built.
The takeaway: if you're in a caloric deficit, compressing your eating window might actively protect muscle — not just avoid hurting it.
Nature Neuroscience · Published May 30, 2026
Lifting weights twice a week actually grows the memory center of your brain
TL;DR
A meta-analysis of 22 trials and over 4,000 participants found that people doing resistance training at least twice weekly showed measurable growth in hippocampal volume — the brain region most tied to memory and most vulnerable to Alzheimer's disease — over 6 months. Aerobic exercise showed benefits too, but through different pathways, suggesting the two work best in combination. This isn't vague "exercise is good for your brain" territory. It's specific, measurable structural change from picking up heavy things.
The takeaway: strength training for brain health deserves the same attention we give aerobic exercise. For people whose joints make running difficult, this might be the more important finding.

Today: Morning Sunlight
Morning Sunlight Exposure
Evidence: Strong Source: Huberman Lab

Andrew Huberman calls this the single most important thing you can do for your sleep, mood, and daily energy — and it costs nothing. Getting outside and exposing your eyes to natural light within 30–60 minutes of waking sets off a cascade of biological effects that most people have never heard of and almost nobody is doing deliberately.

Timing
Within 60 min of waking
Duration
10–30 min outdoors
Cloudy days
Double the time outside
Cost
Free

Here's what's actually happening: light entering your eyes — specifically the low-angle light of early morning — hits specialized cells in the retina called intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells. These cells send a signal directly to the suprachiasmatic nucleus, your brain's master clock, telling it that day has begun. This starts a timer. About 12–14 hours later, that timer triggers the release of melatonin. Earlier and stronger the morning signal, earlier and more robust the melatonin release at night — which means better sleep onset, better sleep quality, and a more consistent circadian rhythm overall. Morning light also triggers a cortisol pulse that peaks around 30 minutes after waking. That sounds alarming but it's actually the healthy kind — it sets your alertness and energy for the day, and front-loads the cortisol your body would otherwise spread throughout the day in a less controlled way.

How to start tomorrow morning

Walk outside within an hour of waking. No sunglasses — the light needs to reach the retinal cells directly through your eyes (looking at the sun is never the goal; just being outside is enough). On a clear day, 10 minutes is sufficient. On an overcast day, go for 20–30. Don't look at your phone while you're out there — let this be the one part of your morning that's just you and the light. If you add nothing else from this protocol, that walk alone will improve your sleep measurably within a week.
